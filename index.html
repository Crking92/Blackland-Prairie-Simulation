What the landscape is showing

The baseline begins as connected prairie with woody vegetation concentrated around waterways, rocky uplands, and scattered motts.

How to explore

The map comes first so visitors can experiment immediately. These notes explain what each part represents after they have begun exploring.

1. Change the era

Move from the pre-European ecological baseline to settlement, modern development, restoration, or a custom future. Every era uses the same underlying hills, rivers, springs, and soils.

2. Watch the drivers

Fire, grazing, succession, disturbance, roads, and invasive plants change vegetation over time. Turn on an ecological-process overlay to reveal the invisible forces.

3. Read the response

Landscape cover, habitat connectivity, ecosystem services, and wildlife indexes respond together. Green-looking land is not always connected or ecologically functional.

Five ways to view the same landscape

This is not a literal reconstruction of any single year. Each era is a conceptual scenario that highlights major landscape processes.

Pre-European baseline

Connected prairie, spring-fed and rainfall-fed waterways, migratory grazing, lightning fire, and Indigenous land stewardship maintain a shifting prairie–savanna mosaic.

Settlement, 1850–1950

Fences, livestock, cropland, rail and road corridors, town growth, and reduced cultural burning begin fragmenting the disturbance system.

Modern corridor

An I-35-inspired transportation spine crosses the rivers at discrete bridge points while subdivisions, commercial areas, turf, stormwater infrastructure, fire suppression, and disturbance-linked invasives reshape the watershed.

Restoration future

The built footprint remains, but prescribed fire, riparian protection, native landscaping, green stormwater infrastructure, and connected habitat improve function.

Build your own future

Adjust urbanization and road density alongside restoration, native landscaping, riparian protection, and green infrastructure to test tradeoffs.

Compare eras

Use the comparison window to place the ecological baseline beside the active era and compare land cover, connectivity, water function, and ecological condition.

A San Marcos–Kyle inspired water system

The water network is deliberately recognizable but schematic. It teaches the difference between spring-fed headwaters, a variable Hill Country river, urban creeks, and stormwater runoff without claiming GIS precision.

Blanco River

Crosses from the western uplands, widens near the Balcones transition, turns south, and meets the San Marcos River downstream. Its flow is highly variable and can rise rapidly during storms.

San Marcos Springs & River

Spring Lake and a visible spring complex create dependable headwaters. Smaller urban creeks add stormwater before the river reaches the Blanco.

Plum Creek corridor

A separate Kyle-area drainage links neighborhoods, parks, open space, and agricultural land. In modern scenarios it carries runoff; in restoration scenarios it becomes a stronger green corridor.

Guided experiments

These short comparisons help visitors move beyond simply watching the animation.

  2. Turn on the habitat-connectivity overlay.
  3. Notice isolated green fragments between roads.
  4. Switch to Restoration Future and compare the connected-habitat score.
Stormwater and riparian protection

  2. Add a rainstorm.
  3. Watch runoff concentrate along pavement and channels.

  2. Keep urbanization high.

  4. See which services recover even when development remains.

What the ecological-process overlays mean

Water flow & runoff

Shows high infiltration on intact vegetated soils, concentrated runoff from impervious surfaces, and the filtering role of wetlands, rain gardens, and riparian buffers.

Fire pathways

Highlights continuous fuels, barriers, wet corridors, and suppression. Mature prairie carries fire much more readily than water, pavement, or heavily wooded riparian areas.

Grazing intensity

Shows where bison or livestock are currently concentrating. Grazing creates a moving patchwork of short grass, bare soil, wallows, and recovering vegetation.

Seed dispersal

Shows likely movement away from woody plants and invasive source areas. Roads, birds, waterways, disturbed edges, and connected habitat influence establishment.

Connectivity & fragmentation

Connected habitat is measured as the largest traversable native network. Roads and urban land divide it even when the total amount of green space appears similar.

Restoration influence

Highlights prairie reconstruction, native landscaping, riparian buffers, green infrastructure, protected remnants, and wildlife crossings.

Model scope and limitations

This explorer is designed to teach relationships, not predict a parcel, flood, species population, fire, or historical condition.

Important: The landscape is a conceptual, regionally inspired model. Locations, dates, rates, species responses, fire behavior, hydrology, and urban patterns are simplified. Wildlife values combine visible habitat with the ecological processes maintaining that habitat; they are relative habitat indexes, not population estimates. Invasive pressure creates a small early risk signal, while expanding invasive cover causes larger declines. Do not use this tool for engineering, regulatory, emergency, archaeological, or land-management decisions without site-specific professional data.
